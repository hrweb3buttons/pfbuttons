Hunter's Toolbox — A Personal Update

Where Things Stand

From Hunter Rodriguez

If you've arrived here from the banner on the main Toolbox page, thank you for taking a moment to read this. What follows is an honest account of where things are, what I've done to try to change them, and what I'm considering if they don't.

I'll be direct with you — because that's how I've always tried to operate with this community, and this moment doesn't call for anything different.

What I built, and why I built it

Hunter's Toolbox started in June of last year as a single tool — a token importer I built for the support team because watching people struggle through a tedious manual process during onboarding felt like a problem I could actually fix. I built it quietly, without being asked, and shared it with a handful of people I trusted.

It spread on its own from there. People shared it. More people started using it. Eventually I made it official because the community had already found it — and by then, what had started as one widget on a Blogspot sidebar had grown into something worth giving a proper home.

I moved it to GitHub Pages, rebuilt it from scratch, and kept adding. The RPC switcher came next, battle tested during a real network outage that left members unable to transact. Then the approval scanner, the calculator, the charts, the swap shortcuts, the mobile fallbacks. The weekly community calls. The guides. All of it built independently, on my own time, out of a genuine belief that this community deserves tools built with real care.

I think of the Toolbox as a love letter to this community. That hasn't changed. What has changed is my ability to sustain it alone, indefinitely, with nothing coming back.

What I've tried

I want to be transparent about the fact that this is not the first time I've raised this issue. I've made the case for sustainability in every way I could think of, over an extended period of time. Here is what that has actually looked like:

1
Donation buttons on the Toolbox itself

Present since early in the Toolbox's life, with written copy explaining the sustainability situation directly. They are still there.

2
Regular community updates

Routine posts detailing changes, new features, and the ongoing investment of time required to keep everything working and improving.

3
Weekly community calls

Hosted every week over Zoom — covering how to use the tools, what has changed, and on multiple occasions, the sustainability issue directly. I walked the community through real world examples of what happens when free infrastructure goes unsupported. I made the stakes as clear as I could.

4
Premium services

I made paid one-on-one support available — program analysis and wallet security reviews — as another way for the community to support the work while getting something specific in return.

5
The story page

A dedicated page on this site telling the full origin of the Toolbox — where it came from, how it grew, and what it means to me — so that the people using it every day could understand the human story behind it.

6
The Public Goods Campaign

A structured series of posts in the community Telegram, making the case through analogy — lighthouses, roads, libraries, gardens, bridges — for why free community infrastructure needs more than appreciation to survive. Fourteen posts across three sets, written carefully and spaced deliberately.

7
Marketplace listings

Individual sponsorship listings on the community marketplace, breaking the infrastructure costs into specific, reachable monthly asks — the domain, the VPS, the RPC node, the Zoom license — as well as a Cornerstone Sponsorship for anyone wanting to cover everything at once.

I've done all of this because I believe the community deserves every opportunity to respond before anything changes. I still believe that. But I want to be honest: the financial response to everything above has been $0. Not a small amount. Nothing.

I'm telling you that not to shame anyone, and not out of bitterness. I'm telling you because you deserve to know the full picture.

Where things actually stand

The Toolbox works. Right now, today, everything on it functions as it should. The tools are there, the calls have been running, the guides exist. None of that has changed yet.

What has changed is my confidence that I can continue investing in it at the current level without any indication that the community values it enough to contribute something back. Two hundred hours of labor. Weeks of campaign posts. Multiple calls making the case directly. A full suite of marketplace listings. A dedicated sustainability page documenting everything in detail.

At some point, continuing to pour effort into something that the community accepts without reciprocation stops being generosity and starts being something I owe to myself to examine honestly.

I am not at the end of my patience. But I am closer to it than I have ever been — and I think you should know that before you find out in a way that is less easy to respond to.

What may happen next

I want to be careful here, because I am not writing this to be dramatic or to issue ultimatums. What I am doing is being honest about where a sustained lack of response leads — because I think you deserve that honesty now, while there is still time to change the outcome.

The Toolbox in its current form requires ongoing time and attention to remain what it is. That time has a cost. If the community continues to consume the tools without contributing to their sustainability, I will eventually reach a point where I have to make decisions about what I can continue to justify.

I don't want to be the next left-pad — a maintainer who disappears one day and leaves the community scrambling. I have been explicit about the risk precisely because I want to avoid that outcome. A controlled, communicated change is better than a sudden one. But a controlled change may still be coming if nothing shifts.

There is more I intend to say on this subject, and I will be saying it soon — here, on the Toolbox itself, in a way that is harder to miss than anything I have done so far. Consider this a heads up.

If you have been meaning to do something and haven't yet — now is a good time. Not because of what might happen, but because the tools are worth supporting, and because the window where that support makes the most difference is open right now.
